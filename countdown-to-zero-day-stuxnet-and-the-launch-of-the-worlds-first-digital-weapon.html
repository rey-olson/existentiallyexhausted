"Smoking guns are hard to find in the counter-terrorism environment; smoking keyboards are that much more difficult."

I am not going to do lengthy book reviews (ain't nobody got time for that - I am already happy that I can finish a book in a reasonable time frame), but I might jot down some thoughts I have while reading the book:

  • Some chapters are unnecessarily long, for example "industrial controls out of control". It is a longwinded chapter and could have been shorter. It does not add much value to the overall story, it feels more like Kim boasting about her investigative prowess.
  • Nevertheless: holy cow, this book is thoroughly researched. Every page has approx. 2 to 3 (interesting!) footnotes. Reading them however really breaks the pace of reading, so I am often pondering: "Should I read the footnotes now or later?".
  • It must be interesting to take a look at the (reverse engineered) Stuxnet source code and/or go through some technical whitepapers discussing its various modules.

Cyberspace is made for plausible deniability.
